Introduction


I worked for a non-profit web development clinic called Serve-IT. My team and I were assigned to work with Rhino’s Youth Center to help them create their new website for their new location and aesthetic. Rhino’s Youth Center is a non-profit organization with the purpose of providing a fun and safe place for teenagers to hang out and express themselves through creative media. This was an extremely rewarding experience, because I got the opportunity to work closely with the client and even volunteer and meet some of the people whose lives they’ve affected.



Presenting Rhino's Messsage Through Colors


Rhino’s wanted a new look. I asked them how did they wanted to present themselves to their visitors. What message did they want to send their users? They gave me some buzzwords to work with: Teen, Fun, All-Inclusive and Modern. I took these buzzwords and began to create some stylings that hit all these points.

First, I focused on color. Color is an amazing tool, because it can say so much about the site without taking up any space on the page. For example, Rhino’s wanted people to know that they are all-inclusive. The rainbow acts as a symbol for this sentiment. For this reason, I decided to include a variety of different colors throughout the site.

I did some searching online and found that they had an older logo that they used to use, which incorporated a variety of color. I used this logo as a model for the color scheme and included a number of these colors throughout the home-page designs. This color-scheme covered two of the four buzzwords: fun and all-inclusive.

We learned through testing, however, that this colorful scheme made the website appear a little too young. We wanted to appeal to teens, not children. I decided that instead of focusing only on the vibrant colors, like red, green, blue, and yellow, it might be a good idea to incorporate the most mature of the colors: black. Black ultimately subtracted from the childish look of the site while not derailing from to the logo’s color scheme. Because the logo would appear on every page, it was important that none of the colors on the page conflicted.



Designing a Modern Web Page


Color covered three of the four buzzwords, but there was one more that still needed to be addressed: "Modern." I researched other modern websites and found that a lot of software and tech websites followed a similar, one-column layout. They would dedicate entire rows to only a couple of elements. For example, Unity and Google AR-Core.

This format proved to be effective for informational sites, or sites that are trying to sell a product or service. While Rhino’s wasn’t trying to sell anything, they were trying to make people aware of who they are and what they do. Therefore, this layout would lend itself well to Rhino’s needs.


The client had never seen site like this, so they were concerned at first about how well it would come across. I showed them a few examples of other sites that utilized this format, which helped them better visualize the end-product and helped put them at ease with this less-familiar, modern layout.


Crafting Many Design Iterations


A good website is never designed in one try. It’s important to rapidly develop multiple versions and layouts to find the best option. These versions can vary slightly, with just a few elements being rearranged, or they can differ dramatically, with the entire layout changing. For example, when designing the home page, my fellow designer and I came up with a few different designs to show the client.


They decided that while they liked the body section better for one layout, they preferred the header, footer, and color scheme of the other. We were then able to design a home page that blended the best of both worlds into a page that the clients were happy with. We weren’t done there, however. We still made consistent tweaks to the page to improve user experience.

It was important to come to a home-page design that we were happy with as soon as possible, because to design the rest of the pages in a coherent fashion, we needed a base style to work off. Once the home-page had a solid first-draft, we could go full force into designing the rest of the site. We tackled pages in pairs, taking on two pages every two weeks. Each page had the same multi-iteration treatment, narrowing them down to finished designs. Occasionally new site functionality would lead to page designs being revised, but this was okay since serious page development hadn’t begun yet.


Site Flow: Breadth vs. Depth



I’m a firm believer in a broad site architecture. The quicker I can get to the page I want to find, the better. There are many different types of users and many different reasons to visit the site. This is why I tried to include a link to every other page somewhere on the home page. These links don’t all have to be on the main navbar, but if someone wants to see, for example, the sponsors page, there’s no reason they should have to first go through the events page.

This site breadth doesn’t persist through deeper pages, however. For example, if I’ve already dived into the programs page, I can no longer reach the booking page in a single click. I would need to either return to the home page or go through the events page. This is okay, though, because if I’m looking at Rhino’s youth programs, I’m probably not planning on booking a show anyway.

Certain pages, however, do need to be reachable from any other page. These pages include programs, events, about us, support us, contacts and sponsors. Links to these pages exist in one of the three places. The Main navbar, the header, or the footer. These three elements exist across all pages and therefor are the containers of all the most important page links.
