Introduction


The Mark Cuban Center for Sports Media and Technology at IU needed a small online arcade of IU Athletics games to be built. The primary purpose of this arcade was to gather email addresses so that the Mark Cuban Center could then advertise tickets for upcoming sporting events to these email addresses. We developed three arcade games over the course of three months: B-Ball Balance, Lil’ 5, and Pigskin Pitch. I also designed and developed the website that houses these games. Encouraging Player Login: High-scores


Since user logins were the primary purpose of this arcade, encouraging users to login was a major point of discussion. What incentive did we want to give our players to persuade them to log into the site and give us their email address? There were multiple routes to explore:
1) Require the player to log in to play the game.
2) Allow 1 or 2 plays of the game before requiring a login.
3) Deactivate some feature(s) of the game, unless the player is logged in.

Each of these options has its pros and cons. After much deliberation, I decided on the most user-friendly of the options: #3. Each player is always able to play the games, whether or not they are logged in, however they will be reminded as they play that they’re missing out on a cool feature (in this case that feature would be a permanent high-score). High-scores are the bread and butter of the 60-second arcade game, and therefore the player would be forfeiting much of the arcade experience by opting out of them. If the player cares about high-scores, they will want to log in. The only downside to this method is that many players might not care, but no method is perfect.


Crafting a Consistent Experience Between Games


These games all exist in the same universe, under one roof: the IU Arcade. Therefore, it’s important that they all feel like they belong together. This effect can be achieved through consistency. Retaining consistent art-style is the easiest way to tie them all together. As you can see, that was certainly a goal of ours. However, there’s another, more subtle method: crafting a consistent flow.

Each of these games shares the same user flow. The player is presented with the main menu. Here, they can either hit the “play” button or alter some minor options. Once the player clicks “play,” they are presented with a quick how-to-play screen, which tells them the basics of how to earn a score. The gameplay then begins. Once they’ve played the game, a game-over prompt is displayed, showing them their high and current scores, as well as the options to continue or to play again. This exact flow is replicated across all the games, contributing toward a consistent flow throughout the arcade.


Designing Mobile-Friendly Gameplay for Max Usability


A large portion of our potential users browses the web on their smartphones. With this in mind, I knew we had to make these games mobile-friendly. That meant designing gameplay mechanics that worked well with both mouse and touch controls. This was an important decision early in the process, as it would shape the games’ core gameplay moving forward.

Touch controls forced us to think “simple” with our designs. While this may sound like a problematic limitation, I believe it pushed us to be more creative with our solutions. How could we make an interaction as simple as tapping the screen for 60 seconds at a time, fun?
Pigskin Pitch utilized skill-based timing: Stop the meters where you want the ball to land.
Lil’5 focused on stamina management: Don’t over-exert yourself, or you’ll regret it.
B-Ball Balance relied on physics-based balancing: Careful, don’t drop it!


Problem Solving: Poor Visuals at Low Resolutions


A major issue we ran into was scaling. Although the aspect ratio of the game window stayed constant, scaling down to resolutions as low as 320x240 caused a serious drop in the quality of the visuals. On top of that, there were major text readability issues. There simply weren’t enough pixels on the screen to display text, in a legible manner.

To try and solve the poor visual quality, we began switching our pixel art to vector graphics, but that caused our performance to suffer tremendously. In the end, instead of suffer performance and frame-rate drops, which would ultimately hurt the player’s ability to get as high a score as possible, I made the decision to sacrifice visual appeal at low resolutions.

We still had the issue of text readability, however, and to solve this problem, I simply changed the text font to be less stylized and easier to render with fewer pixels.


Case Study: Designing Mobile Controls for B-Ball Balance


B-Ball Balance is a game where the player balances a basketball on their finger for as long as they can, while dodging incoming food being thrown at them. To balance the ball, the player simply needs to move their in-game finger around on the screen. On the computer, the tip of the finger follows the mouse. On the phone, the tip of the in-game finger intuitively follows the tip of the player’s real finger.

Movement on the screen was a straight forward design, however I wanted to allow the player to toss the ball up to dodge the food and then catch it again. On the PC, a left click made the most sense to initiate the toss, but the solution wasn’t as simple on the phone. Since the player’s finger is on the screen at all times, tapping isn’t an option. The player could have used a second finger to do the tapping, but then they’d be playing with their thumbs, rather than their index finger. This would have detracted from the feeling of balancing a ball on their finger. The player needed to be able to play the game with just one hand.

I realized that if the ball was in mid-air, there would be a moment when the player wouldn’t need to move their finger around. This meant they could let go of the screen for a moment while the ball was in the air. This led me to design a control in which the act of the player lifting their finger off the screen caused the ball to jump into the air. They would then have some time to place their finger back on the screen before the ball came back down.
