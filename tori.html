Introduction


Tori is a PC game about the exploration of sound. The player takes control of a little bird, named Tori. They fly freely around the land of Oto-Kai, a Shinto-inspired world floating high in the sky above feudal Japan. Tori has the power to harness the kami, or spirits of this world and use those kami to breathe new life into the environment. Each kami produces its own unique sound for the player to enjoy.

Currently, we are about 1 year into production of the 2-year long project that is Tori. Already, Tori has taught me some of the most valuable lessons of my career as a game developer. For over a year, we’ve been designing, developing, testing, iterating, and growing as both a game and a team. What started as a passion project developed by 3 people, has quickly transformed into a 7-person company.


Designing minimal controls for a minimal game.


I designed a control scheme to reflect Tori’s minimal style. This control scheme aims to take advantage of only the mouse. This also keeps the controls as intuitive and simple as possible. We don’t expect our target audience to have a large amount of gaming skill, therefore it’s important that they don’t have to learn an array of keyboard controls to play.

Through rigorous user testing, I discovered that most players prefer non-inverted controls for flight. As someone who played my fair share of flight-based games, this came as a surprise to me. It felt much more natural for me to fly with inverted controls. Luckily, user testing taught me that this not the case for our target audience, so I made decided to make the default controls non-inverted. However, inverted controls are certainly still an option in the options menu.

Now on to the most important control of all: the left-mouse click. This button is the meat and potatoes of our control scheme. It’s context sensitive and allows the player to do many different things under certain circumstances. For example, if you click it while passing through a kami’s area, you will absorb that kami and begin carrying it with you. And if you click it again while you’re already carrying a kami, you will drop that kami. However, if you click it while flying through empty air, you will simply chirp. Believe it or not, this chirp is actually a very important interaction, because it acts as a form of feedback to the player, letting them know that clicking does do SOMETHING, rather than doing absolutely NOTHING.


Creating a User Interface without a HUD.


Early on, I decided that a heads-up-display was going to be both unnecessary for the amount of information we needed to convey to the player, and immersion-breaking, given the airy and mystical nature of our game world. Therefore, I needed to design a User Interface that properly conveyed the necessary info to the player, while remaining in the world space. I quickly realized that our player character, Tori, would be the perfect medium through which to display this information, since the camera view is nearly always focused on Tori in a 3rd-person perspective.

I made a slight modification to Tori’s character model that would both act as an effective UI element and a way to convey Tori’s emotions visually from the back. I simply added a feather that sticks up off the top of Tori’s head. This head feather is easily visible from behind and acts as a great way for Tori to communicate with the player.


Crafting a non-intrusive Tutorial Sequence.


The tutorial sequence is arguably the most important sequence in the game. It is one of the first experiences the player has in the game, after the main menu, and it must tell them how to effectively play the game in an interesting and engaging way. Because this world is designed to feel open and free for the player to explore, we avoided creating a ‘rail-road’ tutorial sequence. Instead, we decided to use the secondary character, the story bird, to guide the player into learning the game’s core mechanics and controls.

Throughout the tutorial segment, the story bird flies to the recommended next step for progressing through the game, continues to fly around that object, whatever it may be, and chirps repeatedly. It’s a way of saying, “you should probably come over here,” but importantly the player is never actually required to follow the story bird.

We used a cross between story-boarding and descriptive writing to explain the step-by-step sequence of the tutorial. First, the story bird guides the player to the Koi’s puzzle stone. This stone, which has a hole through the center of it and the image of a koi fish etched into it, is waiting for the player to fly through with a Koi’s kami. So, if the player decides to follow the story bird and tries fly through without yet having the required kami, they will be rejected and bounced back, as if they hit a wall. This will hopefully tell the player that they did something wrong. Now, the story bird will turn its attention to the kami required, which rests close by.
